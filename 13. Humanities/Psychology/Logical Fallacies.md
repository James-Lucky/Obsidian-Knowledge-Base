## Fallacies of Relevance & Distraction

### Appeal to Authority

- **Alias:** _Argumentum ad Verecundiam_, argument from modesty.
    
- **Definition:** Accepting someone’s argument because of their authority in a field completely unrelated to the argument, rather than evaluating the merits of the claim itself.
    
- > **Example:** My dentist says she’s voting for the conservative candidate, so I will too.
    

### Appeal to Emotion

- **Definition:** Exploiting the audience’s feelings (fear, flattery, ridicule, pity, spite) to convert them to a particular viewpoint.
    

> In some circumstances, appealing to emotion may be appropriate, but writers should avoid it when strict reason and logic are expected.

- > **Example:** I’m sure someone with your vast experience can see that plan B is better.
    

### Attacking the Person

- **Alias:** _Argumentum ad Hominem_, Poisoning the Well.
    
- **Definition:** Discrediting an argument by attacking the character or background of the person making it, rather than addressing the argument itself.
    
- > **Example:** Don’t listen to Becky’s opinion on welfare; she just opposes it because she’s from a rich family.
    

### Appeal to Hypocrisy

- **Alias:** _Tu Quoque_ ("you also").
    
- **Definition:** A specific type of _ad hominem_ argument where a claim is dismissed because the speaker participates in the very behavior they argue against ($A \text{ argues against } B \text{, but } A \text{ participates in } B \rightarrow \text{ therefore } A\text{'s argument is wrong}$).
    
- > **Example:** Jeffrey is of the opinion that the Iraq war is unjust. Jeffrey is himself in the National Reserve, so his opinion is wrong.
    

### Ignoring the Issue

- **Alias:** _Arguing beside the Point_, _Ignoratio Elenchi_.
    
- **Definition:** Shifting the reader’s attention away from the core issue to a completely different, unrelated argument.
    
- > **Example:** No, the suspect won’t say where he was on the night of the crime, but he does remember being abused repeatedly as an innocent child.
    

### Red Herring

- **Definition:** Introducing an unrelated or invalid point to distract the reader from the actual argument.
    
- > [!tip] Relationship
    

> [[#Appeal to Emotion]], [[#Attacking the Person]], [[#Ignoring the Issue]], and [[#Straw Man]] are all variants of Red Herring fallacies.

- > **Example:** Protestors worried about abortion ending lives should spend more time investigating the impact of handguns in childhood deaths.
    

### Straw Man

- **Definition:** Attacking one of the opposition’s minor, unimportant, or weak arguments while completely ignoring their strongest points.
    
- > **Example:** People from Quebec want to secede from Canada to get their own currency. Don’t they realize money isn’t everything?
    

## 🕳️ Fallacies of Presumption & Ambiguity

### Appeal to Ignorance

- **Alias:** _Argumentum ad Ignoratiam_.
    
- **Definition:** Basing a definitive conclusion solely on the absolute absence of knowledge or evidence.
    
- > **Example:** I’ve never seen an alien, so they must not exist.
    

### Appeal to Popular Opinion

- **Alias:** _Argumentum ad Populum_.
    
- **Definition:** Claiming that a position is objectively true or right simply because most people believe it to be true.
    
- > **Example:** The governor has high approval ratings; he is therefore doing a good job.
    

### Begging the Question

- **Alias:** Circular Reasoning, _Circulus in Probando_, _Petitio Principii_.
    
- **Definition:** Using a premise to prove a conclusion when the premise itself already assumes that the conclusion is true.
    
- > **Example:** I know I can trust Janine because she says that I can.
    

### Complex Question

- **Definition:** Combining two separate questions or issues as if they were one, assuming a hidden premise that has not yet been proven.
    
- > **Example:** Why did you steal the CD? _(Assumes as fact that you did, indeed, steal it)._
    

### Equivocation

- **Definition:** Using the same term within an argument but shifting its meaning halfway through.
    
- > **Example:** The sign by the pond said, “Fine for Swimming,” so I dove right in.
    

### False Dilemma

- **Alias:** Bifurcation, Black-and-White Thinking.
    
- **Definition:** Suggesting only two stark choices or solutions to a problem when multiple other valid options exist.
    
- > **Example:** You can either be pro-choice or pro-life; there is no middle ground.
    

### Stacking the Deck

- **Definition:** Trying to prove a point by intentionally focusing only on evidence that supports one side of the argument while completely ignoring the counterevidence.
    
- > **Example:** Summer is the best season because it’s warm and sunny, everything is green, and people can swim outdoors. _(Omits intense heat, bugs, or considerations of other seasons)._
    

## 📐 Causal & Inductive Fallacies

### Composition

- **Definition:** Incorrectly assuming that because individual parts of a whole have certain properties, the entire whole must possess those same properties. _(The exact reverse of [[#Division]])_.
    
- > **Example:** All the parts of the engine were lightweight, so the engine should have been lightweight.
    

### Division

- **Definition:** Incorrectly assuming that because a large body or whole system has certain properties, every individual part must share them. _(The exact reverse of [[#Composition]])_.
    
- > **Example:** That company donates a lot of money to charity, so every person who works there must be a charitable person.
    

### Correlation Implies Causation

- **Alias:** _Cum Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc_.
    
- **Definition:** Concluding that because two completely separate events occur at the exact same time, one must have caused the other.
    
- > **Example:** There was a full moon the night I had my car accident, so I’m never driving again under a full moon.
    

### False Cause and Effect

- **Alias:** Coincidental Correlation, _Post-Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc_.
    
- **Definition:** Claiming that because one event occurred chronological _before_ a second event, the first must have caused the second.
    
- > **Example:** Joan was scratched by a cat while visiting her friend and came down with a fever two days later. The cat’s scratch therefore caused the fever.
    

### Hasty Generalizations

- **Definition:** Reaching a broad, sweeping conclusion based on inadequate evidence or a sample size that is statistically too small.
    
- > **Example:** I liked the last Chinese restaurant I went to, so I will like every Chinese restaurant in the world.
    

### Slippery Slope

- **Definition:** An argument rooted in the fear that taking one relatively minor initial step will inevitably lead to a chain reaction of highly negative consequences.
    
- > **Example:** Embryonic stem cells used for research will lead to full-scale reproductive cloning.
    

## 🧩 Non Sequitur Fallacies

- **Definition:** An argument where the premise used to prove a point is completely unrelated to that point. Literally means "it does not follow."
    

### 1. Affirming the Consequent

Follows a specific faulty formal logic pattern:

$$\text{If } A \text{ is true, then } B \text{ is true.}$$

$$B \text{ is true.}$$

$$\therefore A \text{ is true. (False Inference)}$$

- > **Example:** Dogs are animals. Fluffy is an animal. Therefore, Fluffy is a dog.
    

### 2. Denying the Antecedent

Follows a specific faulty formal logic pattern:

$$\text{If } A \text{ is true, then } B \text{ is true.}$$

$$A \text{ is false.}$$

$$\therefore B \text{ is false. (False Inference)}$$

- > **Example:** If I am a Texan, then I am an American. I am not a Texan. Therefore, I am not an American.
